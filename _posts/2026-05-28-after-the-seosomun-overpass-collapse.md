# After the Seosomun Overpass Collapse

<p class="note-date">May 28, 2026</p>

A few days ago, I heard the tragic news about the collapse at the Seosomun Overpass demolition site in Seoul, where three people lost their lives and three others were injured during a safety inspection.

What struck me even more was that trains carrying many passengers had passed beneath the structure only a few minutes before the collapse. Had the timing been slightly different, the accident could have resulted in an even more catastrophic loss of human life.

In the past, I had the opportunity to work on a structural monitoring system installed on the Seosomun Overpass. While working on similar projects, I also experienced climbing temporary inspection scaffolding beneath bridges and overpasses many times. Because of that, this tragedy felt especially close and personal to me. I could easily imagine the environment, the atmosphere, and the risks faced by the workers on site that day.

Although the monitoring system had stopped operating about a year before the accident, hearing the news still left me with a deep sense of sorrow and regret.

As someone who once worked on monitoring this structure and who indirectly witnessed how close this disaster came to becoming far worse, I sincerely mourn for the victims and everyone affected by this tragedy. At the same time, this accident reminded me once again why I want to continue studying and conducting research: to become a researcher who can contribute, even in a small way, to preventing such accidents from happening again.
